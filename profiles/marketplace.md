# Profile: Marketplace

inherits: base

For two-sided or multi-sided marketplaces: supply and demand sides, liquidity, matching, take rate.

---

## Lean Canvas Overrides

### Customer Segments
A marketplace has at least two customer segments. Define both:
- **Supply side:** Who provides the inventory/service/product?
- **Demand side:** Who buys or consumes it?
- Which side do you solve for first? (Hint: usually the harder side to acquire)

### Problem
Define the problem for each side separately:
- What pain does the supply side have today without your marketplace?
- What pain does the demand side have?
- What friction exists in the current matching process?

### Unique Value Proposition
Must address both sides or clearly pick one to lead with. Example: "The easiest way for [supply] to reach [demand] without [current pain]."

### Revenue Streams
Common marketplace models:
- **Take rate / commission** (% of each transaction — most common)
- **Subscription** (supply side pays to list — e.g. Substack)
- **Featured listings / promoted placement**
- **SaaS layer on top** (tools for supply side — e.g. Shopify)
Push to define the take rate percentage and whether it's sustainable vs. competitors.

### Key Metrics
Marketplace-specific metrics to suggest:
- **GMV** (Gross Merchandise Value) — total transaction volume
- **Take rate** — % of GMV kept as revenue
- **Liquidity** — % of listings that result in a transaction in 30 days
- **Supply concentration** — what % of GMV comes from top 10% of suppliers?
- **Repeat purchase rate** — demand side
- **Time to first transaction** — supply side activation

### Unfair Advantage
Marketplace moats to probe for:
- **Liquidity network effects** — more supply attracts demand, attracts supply
- **Data advantage** — matching algorithm, reputation/review data
- **Exclusive supply** — supply locked in (exclusivity agreements)
- **Geographic density** — dominant in one city/region before expanding

### Channels
- Supply acquisition is often manual early (direct outreach, communities, events)
- Demand acquisition is often SEO / word-of-mouth / referral
- Ask: which side is more supply-constrained vs demand-constrained right now?

## Golden Circle Overrides

### Pricing Strategy
Focus on take rate justification: why would suppliers accept your cut vs. going direct? What do you provide that makes the margin worth it?

---

## The Cold Start Problem

Every marketplace faces this. Ask the founder:
- How do you get the first 10 suppliers without any buyers?
- How do you get the first 10 buyers without any suppliers?
- What's the single-player mode? (Can one side get value even without the other?)

---

## Reference Companies

- **Airbnb** — started with supply (manual outreach to Craigslist hosts), then demand
- **Uber** — geographic density strategy, city-by-city launch
- **Etsy** — supply-first (crafters), then SEO-driven demand
- **Substack** — writer-first, readers follow writers
- **Faire** — cracked supply (indie brands) then unlocked demand (boutique retailers)

---

## Common Pitfalls for Marketplaces

- Treating both sides as equal when one is the bottleneck
- Take rate too high — supply will bypass you once they find customers
- No liquidity in a given geography or category — "thin" marketplace
- Building features instead of solving the cold start problem
- Not defining what "healthy liquidity" looks like as a target
