# Profile: Hardware

inherits: base

For physical products, IoT devices, and hardware+software startups: unit economics, manufacturing, supply chain, distribution.

---

## Lean Canvas Overrides

### Problem
Hardware problems must include the physical pain point, not just digital. Ask:
- What does the user do today with existing hardware or manual processes?
- What breaks, costs too much, or takes too long?
- Is this a new category or a better version of something that exists?

### Solution
Hardware solutions have two layers — define both:
- **Physical layer:** what does the device do? Materials, form factor, key components.
- **Software layer:** app, firmware, cloud platform? What does it enable beyond the hardware?

### Revenue Streams
Hardware revenue models:
- **Direct hardware sale** (one-time — easy to understand, hard to build recurring on)
- **Hardware + subscription** (device at cost or subsidised, revenue on software/service — e.g. Peloton, Nest)
- **Consumables** (device at margin, recurring consumable revenue — e.g. Nespresso, razors)
- **B2B fleet sale** (sell in bulk to businesses — shorter feedback loop than consumer)
- **Licensing / white label** (sell the technology, not the device)

Push: what is the gross margin on hardware alone? (Should be >40% at scale, <20% is dangerous.)

### Cost Structure
Hardware cost structure is more complex. Prompt for:
- **BOM (Bill of Materials)** — component costs per unit
- **NRE (Non-Recurring Engineering)** — tooling, moulds, certification (one-time)
- **Manufacturing / assembly cost**
- **Fulfilment and shipping**
- **Warranty / returns reserve** (typically 2–5%)
- **Inventory carrying cost**
- **Certifications:** FCC, CE, UL, etc.

### Key Metrics
Hardware metrics to suggest:
- **Unit gross margin** (revenue per unit − COGS)
- **Inventory turns** (how fast inventory sells)
- **Return rate** (%)
- **Attach rate** (% of hardware buyers who also subscribe to software)
- **LTV** (especially if consumables or subscription)
- **MOQ** (Minimum Order Quantity from manufacturer — determines cash exposure)

### Unfair Advantage
Hardware moats:
- **Proprietary manufacturing process** or materials
- **Patents** on core mechanism
- **Exclusive supplier relationships**
- **Certification / regulatory approval** competitors lack
- **Data advantage** from connected devices (aggregated sensor/usage data)
- **Brand** (hard to build, very durable once established — e.g. Apple, Dyson)

### Channels
Hardware channels to probe:
- **DTC (Direct-to-Consumer)** via own website (highest margin, full data)
- **Amazon / major retailers** (distribution scale, lower margin, less control)
- **Crowdfunding** (Kickstarter / Indiegogo — de-risks production, builds community)
- **B2B direct sales** (for commercial/industrial hardware)
- **Distributors / VARs** for global reach

## Golden Circle Overrides

### Pricing Strategy
Hardware pricing requires covering COGS + NRE amortisation + desired margin. Common mistake: pricing based on what feels "fair" rather than cost-up + value-down analysis. Ask: at what volume does the unit become profitable? When do you break even on tooling?

## Manufacturing Readiness

Ask the founder which stage they are at:
- **Prototype** (handmade, no tooling)
- **EVT** (Engineering Validation Test — first tooled samples)
- **DVT** (Design Validation Test — near-final design)
- **PVT** (Production Validation Test — manufacturing at small scale)
- **Mass production**

The further left, the more capital and time risk. Flag this clearly.

- **Peloton** — hardware + subscription model, community as moat
- **Withings** — health hardware, DTC + retail, data platform
- **Oura Ring** — subscription attach on premium hardware
- **Anker** — manufacturing efficiency as moat, Amazon-first distribution

## Common Pitfalls for Hardware

- Underestimating time to manufacturing (add 2× to any estimate)
- No software layer — pure hardware is a race to the bottom on margin
- Raising too little for the next milestone (hardware rounds are larger than software)
- No plan for returns, warranty, and defects
- Crowdfunding success ≠ product-market fit — backers are not customers
- Ignoring regulatory certifications until late in development
